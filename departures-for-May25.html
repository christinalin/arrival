RBSP
Earth orbit
Radiation Belt Storm Probes Mission
Launch Date: 08/2012
Will Arrive: 08/2012
 
Part of NASA's Living With a Star (LWS) program, the Radiation Belt Storm Probes (RBSP) will study our planet's radiation belts to help us understand the Sun's influence on Earth and near Earth. Understanding the radiation belt environment has important applications in the areas of spacecraft operation, spacecraft system design, mission planning and astronaut safety.

Mission Goals

  • Gain understanding — to the point of predictability — of how groups of electrons or ions in space form or change in response to changes in solar activity or the solar wind

Fun Fact:

Two spacecrafts are better than one. Only with two spacecraft taking identical measurements and following the same path, can scientists begin to understand how the Earth's radiation belts change in both space and time.
The twin probes study Earth's radiation belts.
(All images courtesy of NASA)

Launch Date: 12/2012

IRIS (Interface Region Imaging Spectrograph) is a planned NASA space probe that will observe the Sun from the Earth's orbit. It will use a special UV imaging spectrometer to study the solar atmosphere, the energy-filled region where the corona and photosphere interact.

Mission Goals

  • Determine how the solar atmosphere is energized
  • Provide new insight into atmospheres around other stars

Fun Fact:

The solar atmosphere is the least understood region of the Sun. IRIS will focus on the chromosphere, the Sun's interface region and use cutting-edge instruments to study density, temperature and the Sun's powerful magnetic field.
IRIS will study the Sun's chromosphere and transition region.

NASA
MAVEN
mars
Mars Atmosphere and Volatile Evolution
Launch Date: 11/2013
Will Arrive: Fall 2014
 
MAVEN (Mars Atmosphere and Volatile Evolution) is a NASA spacecraft currently under development that would study Mars's atmospheric gases, upper atmosphere, solar wind and ionosphere in an attempt to better understand the planet's climate history and potential habitability.

Mission Goals

  • Determine the role that loss of atmospheric gas to space played in changing the Martian climate
  • Determine how much of the Martian atmosphere has been lost over time by measuring the current rate of escape to space

Fun Fact:

MAVEN and its ground-based counterpart, Curiosity, will both try to determine what transformed Mars into the cold, dry, lifeless planet that we see today. By comparing the amount of hydrogen and deuterium, a heavier version of hydrogen, in the Martian atmosphere, scientists hope to understand how much water — and atmosphere — have been lost over billions of years.
MAVEN will study what happened to the Martian atmosphere.
(All images courtesy of NASA)

LDCM, or the Landsat Data Continuity Mission, is the latest in a long series of satellites that have greatly increased our knowledge on how Earth changes over time — particularly climate, the carbon cycle ecosystems, the water cycle and biogeochemistry. It will gather valuable data and images to be used in agriculture, business, education, government and the sciences.

Mission Goals

  • Provide measurements of the Earth's terrestrial and polar regions in the visible, near-infrared, short wave infrared and thermal infrared
  • monitor land and water use around the globe
  • Support disaster response and evaluations

Fun Fact:

For almost forty years, the Landsat program has collected information about the Earth's surface. This data is unmatched in its quality, detail, coverage and length. Their decades of work have allowed people to study the many aspects of our planet and evaluate the changes caused by both natural processes and human processes. Lunar Atmosphere and Dust Environment Explorer
Launch Date: 2013
Will Arrive: 2013
 
NASA's Lunar Atmosphere and Dust Environment Explorer (LADEE) spacecraft will orbit the Moon and use instruments like a dust detector and spectrometers to determine the dust environment on the lunar surface, as well as the composition and density of the Moon's thin atmosphere. This data will help scientists learn more about the Moon's natural environment before it is further altered by humans.

Mission Goals

  • Determine the dust environment on the Moon
  • Determine the composition and density of the Moon's thin atmosphere before it is further disturbed by human activity

Fun Fact:

Fountains of electric dust may be responsible for a mysterious glow seen on the lunar horizon during Apollo 17 and other missions. LADEE will look for the so-called Lunar Horizon Glow and dust in the Moon's exosphere, thought to be carried high above the surface by solar ultraviolet radiation.

Chandrayaan-2

Will Arrive: 2014
 
Chandrayaan-2 is a joint mission between the Indian Space Research Organization (ISRO) and the Russian Federal Space Agency. The mission is comprised of a lander and a rover that will do on-site chemical analysis of the Moon and send the data back to Earth via a lunar orbiter.

Mission Goals

  • Test various new technologies developed by the ISRO
  • Map the Moon's surface in different wavelengths
  • Probe top layers of the lunar surface for the presence of different materials, including water ice
  • Study the lunar exosphere
  • Create a 3-D map for studying lunar mineralogy and geology

Fun Fact:

The weight of Chandrayaan-2, which will include five payloads on the orbiter and two on the rover, cannot exceed 88.2 lbs (40 kg) while the total spacecraft weighs about 5,842 lbs (2,650 kg) at liftoff. During the development process, scientists conduct constant reviews to make sure the spacecraft stays within weight limitations so that it can make it to the Moon.

BepiColombo is a joint mission between the European and Japanese Space Agencies that will consist of two orbiters with 11 scientific instruments, including a gamma ray spectrometer and a laser altimeter, to intensively study the surface of Mercury.

Mission Goals

  • Investigate the origin and evolution of a planet close to its parent star
  • Examine Mercury's exosphere and magnetosphere
  • Determine the origin of Mercury's magnetic field
  • Investigate polar deposits

Fun Fact:

From orbit, BepiColombo will attempt to find water ice in polar craters that are permanently in shadow from the Sun's rays. Water ice strongly reflects radar, and the patches of very high radar reflection near the poles suggest the presence of water ice.

Launch Date: 2018
Will Arrive: To Be Determined
 
Solar Probe Plus is a proposed NASA spacecraft that will study the outer corona of the Sun. The probe will use a heat shield and a special cooling system to protect itself while being so near the Sun. It will also become the fastest ever man-made object as it passes around the Sun at 120 mi/s (200 km/s).

Mission Goals

  • Determine how the Sun's corona is heated and how solar wind is accelerated
  • Explore "dusty plasma" near the Sun and its influence on solar wind and energetic particle formation

Fun Fact:

In 1958, the National Academy of Science recommended a mission to visit the Sun. In 2018, Solar Probe Plus will be the first spacecraft to actually visit our parent star.
The Sun's corona has never been visited by any spacecraft.
(All images courtesy of NASA)

Ice, Cloud, and Land Elevation Satellite 2
Launch Date: 1/2016
Will Arrive: 1/2016
 
ICESat2 is a second-generation orbiting satellite that will use lasers to measure global sea levels, the thickness of polar ice sheets, and vegetation canopy heights to determine large-scale biological changes.

Mission Goals

  • Help us understand global climate change
  • Estimage sea ice thickness
  • Determine polar ice sheet contributions to global sea levels
  • Map land topography and vegetation coverage around the world

Fun Fact:

The only instrument aboard ICESat-2 is called the Advanced Topographic Laser Altimeter System (ATLAS). ATLAS will emit visible laser pulses that create three pairs of tracks, with two miles (3.3 km) between each track. This system allows the satellite to collect more accurate elevation estimates, especially on sloping ground and rough terrain.
ICESat-2 will use a micro-pulse multi-beam approach.
(All images courtesy of NASA)

Part of the European-lead ExoMars mission, the ExoMars Trace Gas Orbiter will consist of two parts: an orbiter and a lander. The orbiter that will measure gasses in the atmosphere and search for a landing site for the ExoMars rover. The static lander will do meteorological research on the surface.

Mission Goals

Fun Fact:

Scientists are interested in studying the presence of methane gas in Mars's atmosphere because the source is either present-day life or geological activity. The orbiter, which is also scouting a landing location for a future rover, and a static lander will relay their findings back to Earth.

JWST
solar orbit
Earth-Sun L2
James Webb Space Telescope
Launch Date: 2018
Will Arrive: 2018
 
The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) is the planned successor to the Hubble Space Telescope. Using its large and very cold 6.5 meter-wide mirror, the JWST will probe deep space in the infrared, looking for the most distant objects in our universe. It will study the formation of the first stars and galaxies and help us better understand how planets form around other stars.

Mission Goals

  • Search for light from the first stars and galaxies that formed in the universe after the Big Bang
  • Study the formation and evolution of galaxies
  • Understand the formation of stars and planetary systems
  • Study planetary systems and the origins of life

Fun Fact:

The strange, diamond-shaped base of the JWST is actually a giant shield that will block light from the Sun, Earth and Moon from reaching the telescope's primary instruments and interfering with observations. To have this work, the JWST will orbit at the semi-stable L2 or Lagrange 2 point. This orbit is about four times farther than the Moon's orbit, but the telescope will keep up with the Earth as it explores deep space.
JWST will orbit 1.5 million kilometers away from the Earth.
(All images courtesy of NASA)
