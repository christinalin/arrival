Deep Impact/EPOXI (Deep Impact Extended Investigation)
Launch Date: 1/12/2005
Arrival Date: 01/2020
 
NASA's Deep Impact probe was designed to study the interior of comet 9P/Tempel, which it did by sending an impactor that collided with the comet's nucleus in 2005. With this success, the probe was given an extended mission — Deep Impact Extended Investigation or EPOXI — that included a flyby of comet Hartley 2 in 2010 and may include, depending on the health of the probe, a flyby of asteroid 2002GT in 2020.

Mission Goals

  • Determine the composition of a comet's nucleus
  • Determine what depth the crater would reach from collision with impactor
  • Determine where the comet orginated in its formation

Fun Fact:

While previous missions had performed flybys of comets, the Deep Impact mission was the first to successfully impact a comet's nucleus. Photographs taken of the impact crater after the debris cleared revealed the comet to be much more dusty and less icy than previously thought.
Material from the comet 9P/Temple is ejected far into space.
(All images courtesy of NASA)

Dawn
Ceres
Launch Date: 9/27/2007
Arrival Date: 7/16/2011
 
A joint American-European project, Dawn is exploring the two largest bodies in the asteroid belt, Vesta and the dwarf planet Ceres, to learn more about the early formation of the Solar System. It will be the first spacecraft to orbit two extraterrestrial bodies — three if you count the Sun.

Mission Goals

  • Capture the earliest moments in the origin of the Solar System
  • Determine the nature of the building blocks from which the rocky, terrestrial planets formed
  • Contrast the formation of two protoplanets, Ceres and Vesta, against other planets so that we understand more about planetary evolution

Fun Fact:

Vesta is the brightest asteroid in the Solar System and accounts for 10% of the entire mass of the asteroid belt. Dawn's first close-up images of Vesta revealed an old, small and battered world covered in craters, bulges, grooves and cliffs.
Dawn began orbiting about 9,500 miles from Vesta.
(All images courtesy of NASA)

NASA's Juno probe will study Jupiter's composition, gravity field, magnetic field, and polar magnetosphere, and will search for clues about how the planet formed.

Launch Date: 1/19/2006

NASA's New Horizons probe will be the first spacecraft to visit and photograph the dwarf planet Pluto, its moons and other icy objects on the frontier of the Solar System. On its current trajectory, New Horizons will leave the Solar System sometime in the late 2020s.

Mission Goals

  • Map the surface composition and temperature of Pluto and Charon
  • Characterize the atmosphere of Pluto
  • Search for an atmosphere around Charon
  • Search for rings and additional moons around Pluto

Fun Fact:

To save on mission costs New Horizons is in a hibernation mode for the majority of its journey to Pluto, waking only once a year for 50 days so scientists can check its equipment and trajectory tracking. Once at Pluto, it will take about 4.5 hours for signals from Earth to reach the spacecraft.

Curiosity

Mars Science Laboratory (Curiosity)
Launch Date: 11/26/2011
Arrival Date: 08/05/2012
 
NASA/JPL's Mars Science Laboratory rover, Curiosity, is the largest vehicle ever sent to another planet. Curiosity will use several sophisticated scientific instruments, like a laser spectrometer and an on-board laboratory, to assess Mars's habitability; that is, whether or not is or ever was capable or supporting life.

Mission Goals

  • Determine whether Mars could ever have supported life
  • Study the climate of Mars
  • Study the geology of Mars
  • Plan for a human visit to Mars

Fun Fact:

Curiosity is equipped with a special "ChemCam" laser that it will be used to study Martian rocks. Once the rover has chosen a potential target, the laser will vaporize a pin-head sized area of rock and turn it into a glowing ionized gas. The ChemCam observes the flash of vaporized rock and analyzes the spectrum of light to identify the elements in the rock.
Curiosity will study objects on Mars with its ChemCham laser.
(All images courtesy of NASA)

GRAIL
Moon Orbit
Gravity Recovery and Interior Laboratory
Launch Date: 9/10/2011
Arrival Date: 12/31/2011 - 01/1/2012
 
The Gravity Recovery and Interior Laboratory (GRAIL) is a pair of washing-machine sized spacecraft that are in similar polar orbits around the Moon. Together, they are conducting high-quality gravitational mapping of the Moon in an attempt to better understand the Moon's structure and composition, the Moon's thermal history and lunar evolution.

Mission Goals

  • Map the structure of the lunar crust
  • Determine the Moon's geological history
  • Study the Moon's internal structure and surface features like impact basins
  • Place limits on the size of the Moon's core

Fun Fact:

There are two instruments on board GRAIL: the Lunar Gravity Ranging System (LGRS), which is the science instrument, and the MoonKam lunar-imaging system which is used for education and public outreach. This camera will allow middle school students all over the country to participate in the GRAIL mission and lunar exploration.
MoonKam captured the Earth above the Moon's landscape.
(All images courtesy of NASA)

As it orbits around the Moon, China's Chang'e 2 spacecraft's high-resolution camera is mapping the Moon's surface. This data will help prepare its successor, Chang'e 3, make a soft landing on the Moon around 2013.

Mission Goals

  • Take high-resolution images of the lunar surface to assist in the selection of a future manned landing site
  • Test the Chinese tracking and control network by moving into the Earth-Sun L2 point

Fun Fact:

The first images captured by Chang'e 2 revealed a crater-filled region in the Moon's northern hemisphere Iridium Sinis (Bay of Rainbows). One primary objective of Chang'e 2 is to scout possible locations for a manned landing by the Chinese Space Agency around 2020.

Aquarius/SAC-D (Satellite for Scientific Applications-D)
Launch Date: 6/10/2011
Arrival Date: 6/10/2011
 
The Satellite for Scientific Applications-D (SAC-D) is an Argentine-built spacecraft containing scientific instruments from several countries. SAC-D studies atmospheric radiation and temperature, aurora and ground-based fires and lights, sea temperatures, and particles and debris in space. Its primary instrument, Aquarius, measures differences in worldwide ocean salinity.

Mission Goals

  • Measure global sea surface salinity
  • Investigate cosmic radiation and space debris
  • Measure temperature and humidity in the atmosphere
  • Image auroras, fires and lights, and a technology experiment involving GPS navigation

Fun Fact:

Almost all of the information on ocean salinity has been collected from ships and been confined to shipping routes. Most of the southern hemisphere has not been sampled at all. Aquarius will measure global ocean salinity every seven days for three years, and explore regions of the world's oceans never before measured.
Aquarius measures ocean salinity all around the globe.
(All images courtesy of NASA)

Launch Date: 04/3/2004
Arrival Date: 03/18/2011
 
MESSENGER, the first spacecraft to orbit the planet Mercury, is a NASA mission that is studying the chemical composition, geology and magnetic field of Mercury.

Mission Goals

  • Determine the surface composition of Mercury
  • Characterize the geological history of the planet
  • Determine the strength of its magnetic field
  • Investigate the presence of Mercury's liquid outer core

Fun Fact:

Traveling to Mercury can be challenging, since the planet is deep within the Sun's gravity well. To slow down enough to achieve orbit while saving fuel, MESSENGER performed a series of flybys near Earth, Venus and Mercury before finally entering a highly elliptical orbit around Mercury.
MESSENGER continues to uncover Mercury's many mysteries.

Solar Dynamics Observatory
Launch Date: 2/11/2010
Arrival Date: 2/11/2010
 
SDO, the Solar Dynamics Observatory, is a NASA mission that is studying the Sun-Earth relationship; more specifically, SDO is trying to understand the near-Earth effects of the Sun by studying the solar atmosphere on small time scales and in many wavelengths.

Mission Goals

  • Understand the Sun's influence on the Earth and near-Earth space by studying the solar atmosphere

Fun Fact:

Did you know there are waves on the Sun? Billions of little ripples move across the Sun, similar to shocks from an earthquake. Using an instrument called the Helioseismic and Magnetic Imager (HMI) to measure the ripples and magnetic field on the surface of the Sun, scientists are figuring out what is going on inside and making all the cool stuff happen on the outside — like solar wind, solar flares and coronal mass ejections.
SDO images the Sun in many wavelengths.
(All images courtesy of NASA)

IBEX, or the Interstellar Boundary Explorer, is a NASA satellite in high Earth orbit that is mapping the boundary between our Solar System and interstellar space. Its instruments are measuring the location and magnitude of charged particles as they collide with the termination shock — the region of space where solar wind slows down.

Mission Goals

  • Determine the edge of the Solar System by mapping the heliosphere boundary

Fun Fact:

The Adler Planetarium is NASA's lead Education and Public Outreach (EPO) institution for the IBEX mission. Adler staff developed all of the IBEX activities, materials, and the IBEX: Search for the Edge of the Solar System planetarium show.

THEMIS

Time History of Events and Macroscale Interactions During Substorms
Launch Date: 2/17/2007
Arrival Date: 2/17/2007
 
Actually five separate satellites, THEMIS studies energy released from Earth's magnetosphere that can become beautiful auroras near our planet's poles. Three of these satellites remain in Earth's magnetosphere, but two of the orbiters — called ARTEMIS P1 and P2 — have been moved to a lunar orbit where they are collecting data on the Moon-Sun relationship.

Mission Goals

  • Study energy releases from the Earth's magnetosphere known as substorms

Fun Fact:

Just as severe thunderstorms on Earth are accompanied by hail and tornadoes, it is believed that some of the most intense space storms — the ones producing the most penetrating radiation — are accompanied by substorms. In this illustration, THEMIS' orbits are represented by orange ovals, blue lines represent the Earth's magnetic field, and the white flash represents energy released during substorms.

NASA/ESA/ASI
Cassini-Huygens
saturn
Launch Date: 10/15/1997
Arrival Date: 07/1/2004
 
Cassini-Huygens is an international mission comprised of two spacecraft, the Cassini orbiter and the Huygens probe. Cassini is tasked with studying the atmosphere and ring system of Saturn, while Huygens successfully landed on Saturn's mysterious moon Titan in 2005 — the first landing on a body in the outer Solar System.

Mission Goals

  • Study the structure and behavior of Saturn's rings
  • Determine the surface structure and composition of Saturn's moons, particularly Titan
  • Measure the planet's magnetosphere and atmosphere at cloud level

Fun Fact:

With 18 scientific instruments, Cassini is the largest robotic deep space probe ever built. Such a spacecraft may never be duplicated, since today's mission design strategies call for smaller spacecraft with more specialized science objectives. Smaller spacecraft are less expensive and easier to design because there are fewer conflicts in the mission objective.

Engineers test Cassini's power systems prior to launch in 1997.
(All images courtesy of NASA)
